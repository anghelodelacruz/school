SAC

Nature

The study of media influence determines how, why and under what circumstances such influence occurs. For the media to have some influence over its audiences, it must communicate with them. This means that there needs to be a relational process of creating and interpreting messages that elicit a response. The persuasiveness and power of the media in society are modelled in communication theories which over time have become increasingly sophisticated as they seek to explain the complexities in the relationships between the media and its audience. These models and theories are derived from research.

In the early twentieth century, concern about the influence of the media centred on propaganda, the popular media and on broadcasting. In the latter part of the twentieth century the concept of addiction to TV, violent films and pornography became the focus of alarm about the media. As markets emerged for various media forms, debate had begun on the influence on its audience. Today, moral panic about the media had shifted from violence to privacy and the health impact of media consumption.

For much of the last century, research into media effects centred on the concept of the passive or active audience. Its focus was on how the text affected the audience rather than on how the audience may interact with and interpret the text. Recent theories however are now interested in uncovering these complexities.

Communication Theories

Hypodermic Needle Theory 1920s-1940s was one of the earliest theories of media influence focussing on the tranmission on the audience. It is a linear communication theory which suggests that the media has a direct and powerful influence on audiences, like being injected with a hypodermic needle. This means that audiences are directly affected by the media they consume. In this theory, audiences are passive and homogenous not acocunting for individual differences.

Semiotics 1970s, this is a method of text analysis that deals with the relationship between a text and the culture that produces or consumes it. Through signs and systems, semiotics examines the nature of meaning within the contexts of media form, production, message and consumption. It suggests that media texts are constructed using a shared code which is encoded by the sender and read by the receiver. In this theory, audiences are active because they construct meaning from texts by 'reading' signs. Meaning varies as signs can have both shared and individual connotations.

These two theories differ greatly and in between the half century that they were developed, it can be seen how complex these theories have become. Hypodermic needle theory is often supported by moral panics such as the Orson Wellees War of the Worlds broadcast in 1938. Its influence in comparison to semiotics is much greater as it assumes audiences are passive. It falls short in the simplistic nature it holds and is often disregarded as an outdated way of thinking about media influence. This is because audiences have become more active than this theory suggests. Semiotics however is on the opposite end of the scale of media influence. As this suggests audiences construct their own meaning, the theory cannot be supported by empirical evidence and there is no way of constructing research. This is a similarity in the sense that both are used as a way of thinking about media influence rather than solid structure of communication between media text and audience. However as one focuses on transmission the other focuses on the role of the reader and how it links our reading of texts with culture and values. As hypodermic needle theory is simple and well defined, semiotics is loosely defined in its approach. The evidence, or the lack there of in each theory is similar as one suggests exaggerated evidence of moral panic caused by the broadcast and the other unable as there is no agreed way of conducting research for such a theory.

In continuity, these theories differ in the academic approaches that the researchers have used. As hypodermic needle theory falls under the category of the effects tradition, it focuses on the effect that media texts have on audiences. Therefore its research lies within experimental studies and quantitative evidence. This differs in cultural studies in semiotics which focuses on the act of communication and the issue of media influence within the context of society and culture. It acknowledges that individual media consumption and construction of meaning exists. Its research lies within surveys, interviews and other qualitative evidence.
